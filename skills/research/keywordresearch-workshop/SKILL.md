# Keyword Research

Zero-cost keyword strategy. Structured thinking replaces expensive software.

## the job

People approach keyword research the wrong way. They fire up a tool, get buried in numbers, and walk away with a spreadsheet that collects dust.

This flips the sequence. Start from the business: what do you sell, who wants it, and what are they typing into Google? Those answers feed a **scored content plan** containing keyword clusters sorted by topic, ranked on actual opportunity, matched to content formats, and topped with a clear first move.

**Deliverable:** Keyword clusters tied to specific content pieces, scored by business impact and realistic opportunity.

The process moves through five stages:

1. **Mine** — Extract starting keywords from what the business actually does
2. **Expand** — Run the Prism Scan to grow a full keyword universe
3. **Cluster** — Sort keywords into topic anchors
4. **Score** — Rate each anchor on opportunity and business value
5. **Map** — Pair clusters with content formats and build a publishing schedule

---

## inputs needed

Hold off on generating keywords until you've confirmed these details:

- **Offer** — What's being sold? (one or two sentences)
- **Audience** — Who's the target buyer? (get specific)
- **Website** — Current URL, so you can understand what content already exists
- **Competition** — Two or three competitors (or help identify them)
- **Goal** — Traffic, leads, sales, or authority?
- **Timeline** — Chasing quick wins or building long-term dominance?

## mine: surface starter terms

Pull 20-30 starter keywords from the business context, spread across four buckets:

| Bucket | What goes here | Example (online cooking school) |
|--------|---------------|--------------------------------|
| Offer terms | Your actual products and services | "online cooking classes", "meal prep course", "home chef program" |
| Pain terms | Frustrations that drive them to search | "can't cook healthy meals", "wasting money on takeout", "boring weeknight dinners" |
| Result terms | Outcomes they dream about | "cook restaurant-quality food at home", "impress dinner guests", "healthy family meals" |
| Industry terms | The broader category you belong to | "online culinary school", "cooking courses", "learn to cook" |

---

## expand: the prism scan

Feed each starter keyword through 6 different angles to surface terms you'd otherwise miss.

| Angle | Focus | Example keywords |
|-------|-------|-----------------|
| **Your offer** | Products, programs, packages you sell directly | "8-week cooking bootcamp", "virtual chef lessons", "knife skills course" |
| **Their frustration** | Problems they're googling late at night | "everything I cook tastes bland", "scared of the kitchen", "keep burning food" |
| **Their desired result** | The transformation, not the process | "cook like a pro at home", "healthy dinners in 20 minutes", "host a dinner party confidently" |
| **Your edge** | Why you instead of thousands of alternatives | "cooking for absolute beginners", "30-minute meals only", "chef who couldn't boil water at 25" |
| **Adjacent interests** | Related topics your audience also explores | "kitchen organization", "grocery budgeting", "food photography", "wine pairing basics" |
| **Names and brands** | People, tools, and methods you want associated with you | "Blue Apron alternative", "Gordon Ramsay techniques", "MasterClass cooking", "cast iron cooking" |

After running all six angles, multiply each keyword using the patterns in [references/topic-mining.md](references/topic-mining.md).

**Goal:** Grow from 20-30 starters to 100-200 expanded keywords.

## cluster: form topic anchors

Take the expanded keyword list and organize it into topic anchors. Each anchor is a broad subject that can support:

- One comprehensive flagship guide (3,000-8,000 words)
- Three to seven supporting articles underneath it
- Room to keep adding content over time

Aim for 5-10 anchors total.

**Gut check:** Could this topic sustain a complete guide covering everything someone needs to know? If yes, it's an anchor.

### how to sort keywords into clusters

1. **Meaning** — Keywords describing the same core concept go together
2. **Intent** — Keywords where the searcher wants the same type of answer get grouped
3. **Pick the anchor keyword** — Broadest term in the group becomes the label
4. **Tag the supporters** — More specific variations sit underneath

### sample anchor

**Anchor:** Quick Weeknight Dinners

**Supporting clusters:**
- 30-minute dinner recipes for families (how-to)
- Meal prep vs cooking fresh every night (comparison)
- Easy dinners with 5 ingredients or less (practical guide)
- Weeknight dinner mistakes everyone makes (problem-solution)
- Best one-pot meals for beginners (listicle)

### the foundation filter

Every anchor needs to clear four gates before you commit resources to it. Skipping this step is the fastest way to write content nobody finds.

**Gate 1: Search demand**
Does this anchor attract more than 1,000 combined monthly searches across its cluster?
- Pass → Valid anchor
- Fail → Downgrade to a single article, or cut it

The trap: Naming an anchor after your signature method. Nobody searches your method name. They search the problem it fixes.

**Gate 2: Audience language**
Are you using the words THEY type, or the words YOU use internally?

- Wrong: "My signature culinary system"
- Right: "Learn to cook at home"
- Wrong: "[Your brand] recipes"
- Right: "Easy recipes for beginners"

Search engines reward content that matches how real people phrase things.

**Gate 3: Competitive reality**
Pull up the top three results for your anchor keyword:
- All major authority sites (Food Network, Bon Appetit, NYT Cooking)? Find a tighter angle.
- Mix of big and small publishers? Winnable with superior content.
- Weak content from unknown sites? Open territory.

**Gate 4: Unfair advantage**
What do you bring that nobody else can?
- Your own recipe data or student results → High priority
- A unique teaching method you built → High priority
- Hands-on experience (you actually cook daily, not just write about it) → Priority
- The same recycled tips everyone publishes → Deprioritize

**Scoring each anchor:**

```
Anchor: [Name]
Search demand: PASS/FAIL — [evidence]
Audience language: PASS/FAIL — [evidence]
Competitive reality: PASS/FAIL — [evidence]
Unfair advantage: YES/NO — [what is it]
VERDICT: VALID ANCHOR / DEMOTE TO ARTICLE / CUT
```

Two or more failures means it doesn't qualify as an anchor. Fold it into a single article elsewhere, or drop it.

## score: the action ladder

Not every cluster deserves attention right now. Rate each one across three dimensions.

### business value

- **High** — Directly tied to revenue. Commercial intent keywords. Close to a purchase decision. Core offer.
- **Medium** — Indirect path. Builds credibility. Captures email signups. Educational content.
- **Low** — Awareness only. Loosely related to the business. Nice to have, not essential.

### opportunity level

Signals pointing to high opportunity:
- No quality content exists yet (you'd own the space)
- Existing content is stale (two-plus years old)
- Top results are shallow and generic
- You have a unique angle competitors miss
- Interest is trending upward (check Google Trends)

Signals pointing to low opportunity:
- Dominated by massive authority sites
- Already packed with excellent content
- Extremely competitive commercial terms
- Declining search interest

### time to rank

- **Fast (3 months)** — Low competition, genuine expertise or unique data, obvious content gap
- **Medium (6 months)** — Moderate competition, needs thorough content, clear path to stand out
- **Long (9-12 months)** — High competition, requires building domain authority, may need backlink work

### combining the scores

- High value + High opportunity + Fast → **First priority**
- High value + High opportunity + Medium → **Second priority**
- High value + Medium opportunity + Fast → **Third priority**
- Medium value + High opportunity + Fast → **Quick win**
- High value + Low opportunity + Any speed → **Long play**
- Low value + Any combination → **Backlog**

## map: assign content pieces

For each high-priority cluster, decide exactly what to build.

### matching format to purpose

- **Flagship Guide** (5,000-8,000 words) — When you want to completely own a topic
- **Step-by-Step Tutorial** (2,000-3,000 words) — Teaching a process from start to finish
- **Comparison Piece** (2,500-4,000 words) — X vs Y articles, "best of" roundups
- **Listicle** (2,000-3,000 words) — Collections of tools, examples, or tips
- **Use Case Article** (1,500-2,500 words) — Targeting a narrow niche or specific scenario
- **Explainer** (1,500-2,500 words) — Answering "what is [term]" questions

### reading search intent

| Intent signal | Keyword clues | Content approach | Best CTA |
|--------------|---------------|-----------------|----------|
| Learning | what, how, why, guide | Teach thoroughly | Newsletter signup, free resource |
| Evaluating | best, vs, review, compare | Help them decide | Free trial, demo |
| Buying | buy, pricing, get, hire | Remove friction | Purchase, book a call |

### publishing cadence

- **Tier 1 (Weeks 1-4):** Highest priority flagship content
- **Tier 2 (Weeks 5-8):** High priority supporting pieces
- **Tier 3 (Weeks 9-12):** Medium priority depth builders
- **Tier 4 (Backlog):** Lower priority items for later

## worked example: online cooking school

### context gathered
- **Business:** Online cooking school with an 8-week home chef program
- **Audience:** Busy professionals 25-40 who want to stop relying on takeout and cook confidently at home
- **Goal:** Leads for the cooking program
- **Timeline:** Blend of fast wins and long-term authority

### starter keywords mined
- online cooking classes
- meal prep course
- quick weeknight dinners
- learn to cook for beginners
- healthy cooking at home
- home chef program

### expanded via prism scan (sample)

**Angle 1 (Your offer):** online cooking classes, 8-week chef program, virtual cooking lessons, knife skills course

**Angle 2 (Their frustration):** everything tastes bland, scared to try new recipes, keep burning food, wasting money on delivery apps

**Angle 3 (Desired result):** cook restaurant meals at home, healthy dinners in 20 minutes, host a dinner party confidently, impress a date with homemade food

**Angle 4 (Your edge):** cooking for absolute beginners, 30-minute meals only, chef who couldn't boil water at 25

**Angle 5 (Adjacent):** kitchen organization tips, grocery budgeting, food photography basics, wine pairing for beginners

**Angle 6 (Names/brands):** Blue Apron alternative, Gordon Ramsay techniques, MasterClass cooking review, cast iron cooking methods

### organized into anchors

**Anchor 1: Quick Weeknight Dinners** (Priority: Critical)
- 30-minute dinner recipes for busy people
- Weeknight meals with 5 ingredients or less
- Meal prep vs cooking fresh: which saves more time
- Common cooking mistakes that slow you down

**Anchor 2: Cooking for Complete Beginners** (Priority: Critical)
- How to start cooking with zero experience
- Kitchen tools every beginner actually needs
- Simple recipes that teach real technique
- Overcoming fear of the kitchen

**Anchor 3: Online Cooking Classes** (Priority: High)
- Are online cooking classes worth the money
- Online classes vs in-person cooking school
- How to choose an online cooking program
- What to expect from a virtual cooking course

### top 3 recommendations

**1. "Quick Weeknight Dinners for Busy Professionals" (First Priority)**
- Perfect audience match
- Competition is generic "easy recipes" content, not targeted to professionals
- Practitioner credibility: real student results and tested recipes
- Flagship guide, 5,000+ words

**2. "How to Start Cooking When You Have Zero Experience" (Second Priority)**
- Large search volume across the cluster
- Current top results are surface-level listicles
- Unique angle: transformation stories from students who went from takeout-only to confident cooks
- Comprehensive guide, 3,000+ words

**3. "Are Online Cooking Classes Worth It?" (Quick Win)**
- Commercial intent, searchers are close to buying
- Thin competitor content in this space
- Natural fit for weaving in student testimonials and course details
- Comparison piece, 2,500+ words

## boundaries

This produces **strategic direction**, not:
- Real-time search volume data (use free tools to verify if needed)
- Automated search results analysis (check manually)
- Finished written content (pass to the copywriting-workshop skill)
- Technical SEO audits (separate discipline entirely)

The output is a scored plan. Writing comes next.

## free validation tools

When you want to sanity-check the research with real numbers:

- **Google Trends** (trends.google.com) — Spot whether interest is climbing or fading
- **Google Search** — Autocomplete suggestions and "People Also Ask" boxes are free gold
- **AnswerThePublic** (free tier) — Pulls question-based keyword ideas
- **AlsoAsked** (free tier) — Shows how questions connect to each other
- **Reddit/Quora search** — Reveals the actual words people use when discussing your topic

## quality check

Strong keyword research output meets these standards:

1. **Has a clear first move** — Not just a list, but a specific "start here" recommendation
2. **Ranked by real opportunity** — Scored and prioritized, not a keyword dump
3. **Honest about competition** — Doesn't pretend every term is winnable
4. **Tied to business goals** — Strategy driven by revenue, not vanity traffic
5. **Includes format guidance** — Content types and angles assigned, not just raw terms

If the deliverable reads "here are 500 keywords, figure it out" — it missed the mark.
