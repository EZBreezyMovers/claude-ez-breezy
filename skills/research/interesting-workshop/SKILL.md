# Idea Sharpener

Most content fails for the same reason: it tells people what they already believe. Nobody shares "yep, that's what I thought." They share "holy shit, I never thought of it that way."

This skill takes any idea and makes it impossible to ignore.

---

## what this does

Takes a topic, claim, or piece of content and runs it through two lenses:

1. **The Angle Lens** — Find what's counterintuitive, surprising, or assumption-breaking about the topic (based on Murray Davis's research on what makes ideas interesting)
2. **The Delivery Lens** — Choose the presentation technique that makes the angle land hardest (14 proven formats from high-performing content)

**Output:** 3-5 reworked versions of the idea, each with a different angle and delivery method. Ready to use in posts, newsletters, hooks, or headlines.

---

## before starting: gather context

**STOP. Do not generate any output until you know these three things. Ask for anything missing.**

1. **The idea or topic** — What are they trying to make interesting? (a blog post, a take, a concept, a product angle)
2. **The audience** — Who needs to find this interesting? (their current beliefs matter — you can only surprise someone if you know what they expect)
3. **The format** — Where will this live? (tweet, newsletter, headline, talk, landing page — delivery changes everything)

---

## step 1: find the assumption

Before you can make something interesting, you need to know what your audience currently takes for granted about this topic.

**The core rule from Murray Davis:** An idea is interesting when it denies something the audience assumes to be true. Not everything they believe — just one specific thing.

| If the denial is... | The audience says... |
|---------------------|---------------------|
| Too weak (confirms what they think) | "That's obvious" |
| Just right (challenges one belief) | "That's interesting!" |
| Too strong (rejects their entire worldview) | "That's absurd" |
| Irrelevant (challenges something they don't care about) | "So what?" |

**How to surface the assumption:**
- What does "everyone knows" about this topic?
- What advice gets repeated so often it's become wallpaper?
- What do experts in this space agree on?
- What would feel risky or uncomfortable to challenge?

The assumption you're looking for is the one that feels so true nobody even questions it anymore.

---

## step 2: pick an angle type

There are 12 ways to deny an assumption. Each one creates a different flavor of "interesting." Pick the one that fits the topic best.

For full details, examples, and both directions of each type, see [references/proposition-types.md](references/proposition-types.md).

| # | Angle Type | The Move | Quick Example |
|---|-----------|----------|---------------|
| 1 | Organization | Chaos is actually ordered (or order is actually chaos) | "Markets aren't random — they follow predictable emotional cycles" |
| 2 | Composition | Many things are actually one thing (or one thing is actually many) | "All productivity advice is actually about managing energy" |
| 3 | Abstraction | Personal problem is actually systemic (or systemic is actually personal) | "Your impostor syndrome isn't personal — it's structural" |
| 4 | Generalization | Universal rule is actually local (or local truth is actually universal) | "What works in Silicon Valley fails everywhere else" |
| 5 | Stabilization | Stable thing is actually changing (or changing thing is actually stable) | "The 'AI revolution' is following the same hype cycle as every tech before it" |
| 6 | Function | Broken thing actually works (or working thing is actually broken) | "Meetings work — you're just running them wrong" |
| 7 | Evaluation | Bad thing is actually good (or good thing is actually bad) | "Procrastination is a feature, not a bug" |
| 8 | Correlation | Unrelated things are connected (or related things aren't) | "Sleep quality predicts startup success better than funding" |
| 9 | Co-existence | Compatible things can't coexist (or incompatible things can) | "You can't have work-life balance AND exceptional success" |
| 10 | Co-variation | More X means less Y, not more (or vice versa) | "The more you optimize, the less you achieve" |
| 11 | Opposition | Similar things are actually opposite (or opposites are actually the same) | "Hustle culture and anti-hustle culture come from the same place" |
| 12 | Causation | The cause is actually the effect (or vice versa) | "You don't write because you have ideas — you have ideas because you write" |

**Combining types:** The strongest propositions often stack two types. "That 'bad' habit isn't hurting your productivity — your productivity obsession is creating the habit" combines Evaluation + Causation.

---

## step 3: check it hits both levels

An interesting idea needs to work on two levels. If it only hits one, add the other.

| Level | What it means | If it's missing, add... |
|-------|--------------|------------------------|
| Intellectual | Challenges something they believe | "This works because of a counterintuitive truth..." |
| Practical | Changes something they should DO | "So what? Here's what to do differently..." |

Pure theory without practical implication = "Huh, cool" then forgotten.
Pure practical tip without insight = useful but not shareable.

Both together = "I need to send this to someone."

---

## step 4: choose a delivery technique

The angle is WHAT you say. The delivery technique is HOW you say it. Both matter.

**The Content Hierarchy** — always aim for Level 3 or 4:

| Level | Formula | Result |
|-------|---------|--------|
| 1 (skip) | Obvious info + obvious delivery | Eye roll |
| 2 (decent) | Obvious info + surprising delivery | "New wrapper" |
| 3 (strong) | Surprising info + straightforward delivery | "Mind blown" |
| 4 (elite) | Surprising info + surprising delivery | "Life-changing" |

Pick a delivery technique from the 14 options. For full details and examples, see [references/novelty-techniques.md](references/novelty-techniques.md).

| # | Technique | When it works best |
|---|-----------|-------------------|
| 1 | Powerful Analogy | Connecting something familiar to something unexpected |
| 2 | "Most People Think X, But Actually Y" | Direct assumption-denial with evidence |
| 3 | Personification | Attaching insight to a memorable character or archetype |
| 4 | Story-First | Leading with a specific moment before revealing the lesson |
| 5 | Preframing | Borrowing credibility from a source before sharing the insight |
| 6 | Weird Specific Names | Creating curiosity through unusual naming |
| 7 | Strategic Ridicule | Using absurd numbers or titles for attention |
| 8 | Outcome over Mechanism | Leading with what they get, not how it works |
| 9 | Scientific Anchor | Opening with an unknown research finding |
| 10 | Ancient Wisdom | Connecting to historical stories or concepts |
| 11 | Strategic Tension | Building conflict between two positions, then resolving |
| 12 | Strategic Paradox | Highlighting genuine contradictions without easy answers |
| 13 | Illuminating Question | Letting the reader arrive at the conclusion themselves |
| 14 | "Screw the Obvious" | Listing and dismantling all the cliche advice first |

---

## step 5: construct and test

Build the reworked idea by combining the angle (from step 2) with the delivery technique (from step 4).

**Before delivering, run these three checks:**

1. **The Surprise Test** — If you showed this to someone in the target audience, would they pause? Not agree or disagree — just pause. That's the signal.
2. **The Share Test** — Would someone send this to a friend or colleague? Interesting ideas create the urge to say "you need to see this."
3. **The "So What" Test** — Does this change what the reader thinks, feels, or does? If it's just clever without consequence, sharpen it.

## worked example: making "consistency is key" interesting

### Context
- **The idea:** "Being consistent with content creation leads to success"
- **The audience:** Content creators and solopreneurs who've heard this advice 1,000 times
- **The format:** LinkedIn post / newsletter hook

**Audience assumption:** "If I just post consistently, growth will come."

---

**Version 1: The Consistency Trap**

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Angle type | Function (effective → ineffective) |
| The denial | Consistency without direction makes you consistently forgettable |
| Delivery technique | "Screw the Obvious" (#14) |
| Content hierarchy level | 3 |

**The reworked idea:**
> Everyone says "just be consistent." Consistent at what? Posting mediocre content daily? That's how you train your audience to scroll past you. I watched someone post every single day for 2 years and flatline at 400 followers. Then they stopped, spent 3 weeks studying what actually resonated, came back posting twice a week, and hit 10k in 4 months. Consistency is the answer to the wrong question. The right question is: consistent at what?

**Why it works:** The audience already believes consistency is the path. Challenging it creates friction. The specific example (400 followers → 10k) provides the practical proof.

---

**Version 2: The Bamboo Reframe**

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Angle type | Stabilization (changing → stable) |
| The denial | What looks like "overnight success" is actually consistent underground work — but not the kind people mean |
| Delivery technique | Powerful Analogy (#1) |
| Content hierarchy level | 3 |

**The reworked idea:**
> Bamboo spends 5 years growing roots underground. Nothing visible. Then it shoots up 90 feet in 6 weeks. Content works the same way — but the "roots" aren't posting. They're reading your audience's comments, studying what makes them angry, figuring out what they believe that's wrong. The visible output is the least important part of consistency.

**Why it works:** The analogy reframes consistency from "output frequency" to "input depth." It affirms their belief in consistency while completely changing what it means.

---

**Version 3: The Causation Flip**

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Angle type | Causation (reverse) |
| The denial | Consistency doesn't create success — early signs of success create consistency |
| Delivery technique | Scientific Anchor (#9) |
| Content hierarchy level | 4 |

**The reworked idea:**
> Researchers studying habit formation found something uncomfortable: people don't become consistent and THEN see results. They see small results and THEN become consistent. The dopamine hit of a post that lands keeps you coming back. Which means the real strategy isn't "be consistent" — it's "find what works fast, even if it means trying 50 different things in a month." Consistency is the effect, not the cause.

**Why it works:** Reverses the causal arrow. The audience believes consistency → success. This says success → consistency. It also gives them permission to experiment instead of grinding, which feels like relief.

---

**Version 4: The Paradox**

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Angle type | Co-variation (positive → negative) |
| The denial | The more consistently you post, the less creative energy you have for posts worth reading |
| Delivery technique | Strategic Paradox (#12) |
| Content hierarchy level | 4 |

**The reworked idea:**
> The consistency paradox nobody talks about: the more you post, the more your creative well runs dry. But the less you post, the less the algorithm shows you. So you either burn out creating or fade out resting. The creators who actually win aren't consistent in frequency — they're consistent in recovery. They batch-create during creative peaks and go silent during valleys. The schedule follows their energy, not the other way around.

**Why it works:** Names a real tension the audience feels but hasn't articulated. No clean answer — just a better framework. Paradoxes are inherently shareable because people want others to wrestle with them too.

---

**Version 5: The Identity Play**

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Angle type | Opposition (similar → opposite) |
| The denial | "Consistent creator" and "productive creator" look the same from outside but are opposite strategies |
| Delivery technique | Strategic Tension (#11) |
| Content hierarchy level | 3 |

**The reworked idea:**
> Meet Creator A: posts every day, never misses, 365 posts per year. 200 followers gained. Meet Creator B: posts twice a week, but spends 3x more time on each post. 80 posts per year. 5,000 followers gained. From outside they're both "consistent." But A is consistent at showing up. B is consistent at being worth showing up for. Same word. Opposite strategies. One builds a posting habit. The other builds an audience.

**Why it works:** The side-by-side comparison makes the distinction impossible to miss. The audience immediately identifies as Creator A and feels the pull toward Creator B.

---

**Strongest option:** Version 3 (The Causation Flip) for maximum shareability — it reverses a universal belief with a research anchor, which gives people a reason to share it ("look what I found"). Version 1 works best if the audience is already skeptical of generic advice.

## the test

Before delivering, verify each version passes:

1. **Specific assumption denied** — Can you name the exact belief being challenged? If it's vague, it won't land.
2. **One denial, not many** — Challenging one belief is interesting. Challenging everything is absurd. Check you're not overreaching.
3. **Both levels present** — Does it have intellectual insight AND practical implication? Both required.
4. **Content hierarchy 3+** — Is either the info or the presentation (ideally both) non-obvious?
5. **Audience-calibrated** — Would THIS specific audience find it surprising? What's obvious to experts is revelatory to beginners, and vice versa.
6. **Not just contrarian** — Is there a real insight behind the denial? Disagreeing for shock value is empty.
7. **Passes the share test** — Would someone tag a friend or forward this?

If any version is just "clever" without substance, or just "true" without surprise — cut it and try another angle type.
