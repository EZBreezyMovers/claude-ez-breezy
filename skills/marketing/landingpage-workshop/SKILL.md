# Sales Page Builder

Most sales pages fail because people write them top to bottom. They start with the headline, get stuck, and quit. Or they copy some template and end up with generic copy that sounds like everyone else.

The trick is writing in the wrong order. You write the story first (it's easiest and finds your voice), then the offer, then the close, then the lead, and the headline dead last — because by then you actually know what you're selling and why it matters.

This skill builds a complete sales page using that backwards process.

---

## the core job

Produce a **complete sales page** with all 5 sections:
1. Headline (prehead + main headline + deck copy)
2. Lead (150-200 word teaser)
3. Story (credibility through narrative)
4. Offer (what they get, broken into components)
5. Close (price justification + guarantee + CTA + FAQ)

**Output:** Full sales page copy, assembled in reading order, ready to paste into a page builder.

---

## before starting: gather context

**STOP. Do not write any copy until all context fields below are confirmed with the user. Ask for any missing fields before proceeding. Even if the user gives you a rough draft or notes, you must still confirm these fields before writing.**

1. **What are you selling?** — Product name, format (course, workshop, service, ebook), and one-sentence description (shapes the entire page)
2. **Who is it for?** — Specific audience, not "everyone." What do they struggle with? What have they tried? (drives the headline and lead)
3. **What's the main outcome?** — The single biggest result they'll get. Not five outcomes. One. (becomes the headline promise)
4. **What's the price?** — Exact number. Plus any tiers, early bird pricing, or payment plans (shapes the close)
5. **What proof do you have?** — Testimonials, results, credentials, numbers, social proof. Be specific. (goes in story + offer + close)
6. **What's included?** — Every component, module, bonus. What do they literally receive? (builds the offer section)

---

## the process

```
RESEARCH → STORY → OFFER → CLOSE → LEAD → HEADLINE → ASSEMBLE → CHECK
```

You write in this order. The page reads in a different order. This is intentional — each step informs the next.

| You write... | It appears as... |
|-------------|-----------------|
| 1. Research | (doesn't appear — it informs everything) |
| 2. Story | Section 3 on the page |
| 3. Offer | Section 4 on the page |
| 4. Close | Section 5 on the page |
| 5. Lead | Section 2 on the page |
| 6. Headline | Section 1 on the page |

---

## The Core Truth

Every sales page comes down to two things:
1. **Promise** - What outcome are you offering?
2. **Proof** - Why should they believe you can deliver it?

If you're stuck on any section, ask yourself: "Am I making a promise here? Am I providing proof? Am I doing both?"

---

## Step 1: Research the audience

Before writing a word, map these four things:

| Element | Question to answer |
|---------|-------------------|
| **Pain** | What specific problem keeps them up at night? |
| **Failed solutions** | What have they already tried that didn't work? |
| **Desire** | What does their ideal outcome look like? |
| **Objection** | What's the #1 reason they'd say no? |

Present back to the user and confirm before proceeding.

---

## Step 2: Write the STORY

Start here because it's easiest and sets the voice.

### Part A: Introduce Yourself

Your name. Your relevant credentials. Why they should listen to you. Keep it short — just establish enough credibility to make them trust what comes next.

### Part B: The Problem

Paint a vivid picture of the problem. Show how you tried and failed to fix it. Describe the trigger point.

**Structure:**
1. Describe the problem vividly
2. "So I tried [solution 1]... but [why it failed]"
3. "Then I tried [solution 2]... but [why it failed]"
4. "I even tried [solution 3]... but [why it failed]"
5. "That's when I realized [trigger point insight]"

The "tried and failed" section is crucial. It shows you understand their pain because you lived it. It also disqualifies the obvious solutions they've probably already tried.

### Part C: Finding the Solution

**Structure:**
1. "So I started [research/experimentation]"
2. "I discovered/created [the solution]"
3. "I tested it and [specific results]"
4. "Then other people started asking about it"
5. "So I decided to turn it into [the product]"

### Story Tips

- **Use specifics.** "200 emails" is better than "a lot of emails." "$800" is better than "almost nothing."
- **Include the failures.** Skip them and you sound like a guru who's never struggled.
- **Keep it moving.** Don't get lost in backstory. Hit the key beats and move on.
- **End with transition to the offer.** "And that's what became [Product Name]" sets up the offer perfectly.
- 300-500 words total.

## Step 3: Build the OFFER

Now introduce what you're selling. Start with the big picture, then drill into specifics.

### Part A: Introduce It

State the offer name. Show a quality image/mockup if you have one. Explain what it is in one sentence.

### Part B: The Big Promise

Cover these elements:
- The main promise/outcome
- What makes it different (differentiation)
- Timeframe to results
- Handle objections
- Why it's easy/quick
- Proof

### Part C: The Specifics

Break the offer into components. For each element:
- Give it a name (and ideally an image)
- State the promise for that element
- List 3-9 specific benefits as bullets

**Format:**
```
[Module/Component Name]

[One sentence describing what this delivers]

What's inside:
- [Specific benefit 1]
- [Specific benefit 2]
- [Specific benefit 3]
- [Specific benefit 4]
```

### Part D: Testimonials

Include 3-5 testimonials. Not a wall of 20.

For each testimonial:
- Full name and photo
- Location if possible
- Pull the best phrase as a headline above it

**Format:**
```
"[Best quote from testimonial as headline]"

[Full testimonial text]

- [Full Name], [Location/Title]
```

**Testimonial Tips:**
- Use real names and real photos
- Specific results ("4.7% conversion") beat vague praise ("really helped me")
- Keep them short. Pull the best 2-3 sentences.
- Variety is good - show different types of people getting different types of results

## Step 4: Write the CLOSE

The close pushes people off the fence. By now, most readers have already decided whether they're interested. This section converts the "maybe" people.

### Part A: Price Justification

**Step 1: Value Anchor** — What would this normally cost? What have others paid? What's the value of the result?

**Step 2: Introduce the Price** — State the actual price clearly.

**Step 3: Explain Why That Price** — Why is it this amount? What's your reasoning?

**Step 4: Trivialize the Price** — Compare to something they already spend money on without thinking.

### Part B: Guarantee

A good guarantee removes risk and resells the benefits.

**Elements of a Strong Guarantee:**
1. **Give it a name** - Makes it memorable and signals you've thought about it
2. **Make it longer than expected** - 60 days, not 30. 365 days if you can.
3. **Resell the benefits** - Remind them what they're getting
4. **Be positive** - Focus on their success, not the refund
5. **Go beyond just money** - Offer something extra if possible
6. **Make refunds easy** - No hoops, no questions, no hassle
7. **No fine print** - Nothing hidden

### Part C: CTA (Call to Action)

The CTA tells them exactly what to do next.

**Elements:**
- Brief recap of what they're getting (recap close)
- If/then structure
- Button text that's a benefit, not just "Buy Now"

**Button Text — Don't use:** "Buy Now," "Submit," "Purchase"

**Button Text — Use:** "Yes, I Want [Benefit]," "Get Instant Access," "Join The Workshop," "Save My Spot," "Enroll Now - $297"

### Part D: FAQ

The FAQ handles remaining objections. It's your last chance to convert fence-sitters.

**Standard Questions to Include:**
1. **What exactly is this?** - Sums up the offer one more time
2. **Is this for me? / Who is this for?** - Lets them self-identify
3. **How is this different?** - Differentiates from alternatives
4. **What if I don't have [time/money/experience]?** - Handles main objections
5. **When can I expect results?** - Sets expectations
6. **What if it doesn't work for me?** - Restates the guarantee

**FAQ Tips:**
- Put the most important questions first
- Keep answers short (2-4 sentences)
- Use the first question to summarize the offer
- Use the last question to restate the guarantee
- If the FAQ is long (7+ questions), add a CTA button in the middle
- End with another CTA button after the FAQ

## Step 5: Write the LEAD

The lead is the next 150-200 words after the headline. Think of it as a teaser trailer for your sales page. Its job is to get them excited to read the rest. Don't give away all the details yet. Tease them.

### The Lead Formula (8 Steps)

1. **Call them out** — Identify exactly who this is for. Be specific. Make them feel seen.
2. **Make them a promise** — State the big outcome they want. Be bold but believable.
3. **Within a timeframe** — Tell them when they can expect results. Be specific.
4. **How it works (brief mechanism)** — Give them a taste of what makes this different. Don't explain everything yet.
5. **Handle objections** — Address their biggest doubts before they think them.
6. **Let them know it's easy and/or quick** — Remove friction. Make it feel achievable.
7. **Add proof** — Give them a reason to believe. Numbers, results, social proof.
8. **Transition out** — Bridge to the story. Create a natural handoff.

### Lead Tips

- **Tease, don't tell.** Don't explain everything here. Save the details for the Offer section.
- **If you have a strong story, tease it.** "Let me tell you about the email that changed everything..." builds curiosity.
- **Match the energy of the headline.** If your headline is bold, your lead should be bold.
- **150-200 words.** Don't go longer. The lead should be quick to read.

## Step 6: Write the HEADLINE

Write this LAST. It's the most important part of your sales page. Spend the most time here. Its only job is to get people reading. Not to sell. Not to convince. Just to get them to read the next line.

### Component 1: The Prehead

The prehead sits above the main headline. It calls out your target market or their problem directly. No subtlety needed.

**5 prehead options:**
- **Option A: Call out the audience** — "For copywriters who hate selling..."
- **Option B: Call out the problem** — "Struggling to get clients?"
- **Option C: Lead with proof/credibility** — "From the creator of [known product]..."
- **Option D: Create urgency** — "Doors close Friday..."
- **Option E: Set up the main headline** — "Finally..."

You can combine these.

### Component 2: The Main Headline

This is your big promise. Use this formula:

```
How to go from [where they are now] to [where they want to be] in [timeframe] - without [main objection] / even if [limiting belief]
```

**The four pieces:**

1. **Where they are now (the painful current state)** — Be specific about the pain. Use their words.
2. **Where they want to be (the desired outcome)** — Paint the picture of success. Be concrete.
3. **How fast (specific timeframe)** — Give them a concrete timeline. Be believable.
4. **Handle the knee-jerk objection:**
   - Use "without" for external obstacles
   - Use "even if" for internal limiting beliefs

### Alternative Main Headline Formulas

**The Promise Headline:** `[Outcome] in [Timeframe]`

**The Problem Headline:** `Why [Problem] (And What To Do About It)`

**The Story Headline:** `How [Person] [Achieved Result] in [Timeframe]`

**The Intrigue Headline:** `The [Adjective] [Method/Thing] That [Result]`

### Component 3: The Deck Copy

The deck copy sits below the main headline. It explains HOW you deliver on the promise and WHY they should believe you.

**Formula:**
```
Using [mechanism/method name] that [proof of results]
```

For complete headline examples and assembled headline+deck combinations, see [references/examples.md](references/examples.md).

## Step 7: Assemble the page

Put it all in reading order:

```
1. HEADLINE (prehead → main headline → deck copy)
2. LEAD (150-200 word teaser)
3. STORY (introduce → problem → solution)
4. OFFER (introduce → big promise → specifics → testimonials)
5. CLOSE (price → guarantee → CTA → FAQ → final CTA)
```

Add CTA buttons throughout — not just at the end. Put one after the offer section and one after the FAQ minimum.

---

## Step 8: Pre-Flight Checklist

Before publishing any sales page, verify:

- [ ] Headline makes a clear promise
- [ ] Deck copy includes proof
- [ ] Lead is 150-200 words (not longer)
- [ ] Story includes "tried and failed" section
- [ ] Story ends with transition to offer
- [ ] Offer is broken into components with bullets
- [ ] Each component has 3-9 specific benefits
- [ ] 3-5 testimonials (not a wall)
- [ ] Price is anchored against higher value
- [ ] Guarantee has a name
- [ ] Guarantee restates benefits
- [ ] CTA button is a benefit, not "Buy Now"
- [ ] FAQ first question sums up offer
- [ ] FAQ last question restates guarantee
- [ ] Multiple CTA buttons throughout (not just at the end)

## the test

A good sales page:

1. **Opens with a throat-grab** — The headline earns the next line. If the reader doesn't feel "this is about me" in 3 seconds, it failed.
2. **Teases before it tells** — The lead creates desire without explaining everything. If it's over 200 words, it's doing too much.
3. **Builds credibility through story** — The "tried and failed" section proves you understand their pain. No failures = guru pitch.
4. **Breaks the offer into pieces** — Named components with specific benefits. One big paragraph = nobody reads it.
5. **Closes with confidence** — Price justified, guarantee named, CTA clear. "Buy Now" button = lazy.
6. **Sounds like a conversation** — Read it out loud. If it sounds like a brochure, rewrite it.
7. **Has multiple CTAs** — At least 3 buttons throughout. Not just one at the bottom.

If the sales page reads like a template with blanks filled in — it failed. The frameworks are scaffolding. The finished page should feel personal and specific.
